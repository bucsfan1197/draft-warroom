Aging curves — dynasty value vs peak

Each line is what a player at that age is worth over your whole window, against the best age at his position — so it moves when you change contend/balanced/rebuild. Measured from 11 seasons (2014–2025): for a startable player at each age, how much of his production he actually delivered in later years, counting players who washed out of the league as zero. Two findings worth knowing, because both contradict the usual advice. QBs age far more gently than anyone — a 30-year-old keeps 96% of a 23-year-old's forward value over five years, where a WR or RB keeps about 43%. And "RBs age fastest" is a myth: RBs and WRs decline at nearly the same rate (0.43 vs 0.44 from 23 to 30). The real RB problem is the level — an RB is worth less than a WR at every single age. Age still costs a QB, but the damage lands in years five through eight, which is exactly where a contender's window has already closed.

What you get if you wait

These are the players left over if you skip a position entirely — the best one at each spot that nobody in your league is starting. That makes each row a target: drafting a quarterback only helps you as much as he beats the quarterback in the QB row, because that one is free.

It's also why the same points are worth different amounts at different positions. If the leftover running back scores 180 and the leftover receiver scores 210, then a 250-point back is a bigger prize than a 250-point receiver — he's beating a weaker free option by more.
